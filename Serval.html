Serval

Scientific Name:
Leptailurus serval

Common Name:
Serval

Physical Description:
Servals are medium-sized wild cats with long legs, large ears, and a slender body. Their golden coat has black spots and stripes, providing excellent camouflage. They are built for jumping and quick movement, making them skilled hunters.

Where It Is Found / Habitat:
Found in Ethiopian savannahs, wetlands, and grasslands. They prefer areas with tall grasses and water sources, allowing them to hunt small mammals, birds, and insects efficiently.

Behavior:
Solitary and territorial. Servals are primarily nocturnal but may be active during the day in undisturbed areas. They hunt by stalking and pouncing, using keen hearing to detect prey.

Diet and Feeding Habits:
Carnivorous, feeding on rodents, birds, frogs, and insects. They can leap up to 3 meters to catch birds in flight and are known for precision hunting techniques.

Reproduction System:
Breeding occurs year-round. Females give birth to 1–4 kittens after a gestation of about 74 days. Kittens stay with the mother until capable of independent hunting.

Life Cycle and Longevity:
Servals live up to 10 years in the wild. Juveniles learn hunting skills from their mother. Sexual maturity occurs at 12–18 months.

Predators and Threats:
Leopards, eagles, and humans pose threats. Habitat loss and hunting reduce populations.

Ecological Role / Usefulness:
Servals control rodent populations, reducing crop damage. They are indicators of healthy wetland and grassland ecosystems.

Cultural and Historical Significance:
Admired for agility and hunting skill, they feature in Ethiopian folklore as clever and swift animals. They contribute to eco-tourism appeal.

Conservation Status:
Least Concern, though habitat protection is important for long-term survival.

Interesting Facts:
Servals have the longest legs proportionally of any cat. They use their large ears to detect prey movements underground or in tall grass. They can jump high to catch birds mid-air.
