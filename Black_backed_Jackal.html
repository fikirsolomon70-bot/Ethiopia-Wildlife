Black-backed Jackal

Scientific Name:
Canis mesomelas

Common Name:
Black-backed Jackal

Physical Description:
Medium-sized canids with reddish-brown fur, black saddle along the back, and white underparts. They have bushy tails, pointed ears, and sharp teeth for hunting. Agile and alert, they are well-adapted to open and semi-arid habitats.

Where It Is Found / Habitat:
Found in Ethiopian savannahs, grasslands, and semi-arid regions. They occupy areas with sparse cover, burrows, and nearby water sources. Adaptable to human-altered landscapes.

Behavior:
Solitary or small family groups. They are mainly nocturnal but may hunt during the day. Jackals are territorial and communicate through vocalizations, scent marking, and body postures.

Diet and Feeding Habits:
Omnivorous, feeding on rodents, birds, insects, fruits, and carrion. They scavenge opportunistically and are effective hunters for small prey.

Reproduction System:
Breeding occurs seasonally. Females give birth to 2–5 pups after a gestation of 60–65 days. Both parents care for young, teaching hunting skills and social behavior.

Life Cycle and Longevity:
Live up to 10 years in the wild. Sexual maturity occurs at 1–2 years. Survival depends on adaptability and cooperation within family units.

Predators and Threats:
Lions, leopards, hyenas, and humans. Habitat loss and hunting threaten local populations.

Ecological Role / Usefulness:
Control rodent and insect populations. They are important scavengers and maintain ecological balance in savannah ecosystems.

Cultural and Historical Significance:
Black-backed Jackals appear in Ethiopian folklore as clever and opportunistic animals. They are admired for cunning and resourcefulness.

Conservation Status:
Least Concern. Populations remain stable in protected areas.

Interesting Facts:
Jackals can run long distances while hunting and often follow large predators to scavenge leftovers. They are monogamous and form strong pair bonds.
