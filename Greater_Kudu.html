Greater Kudu

Scientific Name:
Tragelaphus strepsiceros

Common Name:
Greater Kudu

Physical Description:
The Greater Kudu is a large antelope with a striking gray-brown coat and vertical white stripes along its torso. Males have long, twisted horns that can reach up to 1.8 meters, while females are hornless. Both sexes have large ears adapted for detecting predators.

Where It Is Found / Habitat:
Found in Ethiopian woodlands, savannahs, and dry forests. They prefer areas with dense vegetation for cover and open spaces for grazing. Protected areas like national parks provide vital habitats for their survival.

Behavior:
Mostly active during early morning and late afternoon. Males may be solitary or form bachelor groups, while females and young form herds of 6–20 individuals. They communicate through visual signals, grunts, and snorts.

Diet and Feeding Habits:
Herbivorous, feeding on leaves, shoots, grass, and fruits. They are browsers rather than grazers and often feed at dawn and dusk.

Reproduction System:
Breeding occurs seasonally, with males competing for females through displays of strength. Females give birth to a single calf after about 7–8 months gestation. Calves remain hidden for weeks before joining the herd.

Life Cycle and Longevity:
Greater Kudus live up to 12–15 years in the wild. Juveniles develop alertness and agility from the herd. Sexual maturity occurs at 2–3 years for females and 4–5 years for males.

Predators and Threats:
Lions, leopards, hyenas, and humans are main threats. Habitat loss and hunting for horns and meat reduce their populations.

Ecological Role / Usefulness:
They maintain vegetation balance and serve as prey for large predators. Their presence indicates a healthy ecosystem, and they contribute to eco-tourism.

Cultural and Historical Significance:
In Ethiopia, kudu horns are sometimes used in traditional ceremonies. Greater Kudus are admired for their beauty and strength and appear in local folklore.

Conservation Status:
Least Concern, though local populations can decline without protected habitats.

Interesting Facts:
Male kudus use their impressive horns in dominance displays. They can jump over 2 meters high to escape predators and are excellent climbers in rocky terrains.
