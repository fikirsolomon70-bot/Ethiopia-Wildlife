Hippopotamus

Scientific Name:
Hippopotamus amphibius

Common Name:
Hippopotamus

Physical Description:
Large, barrel-shaped mammals with thick gray-brown skin, short legs, and massive jaws. Adults can weigh up to 3,000 kg. They have nostrils, eyes, and ears on top of their heads for semi-aquatic living.

Where It Is Found / Habitat:
Found in rivers, lakes, and wetlands of Ethiopia. They prefer slow-moving water and nearby grasslands for grazing. Protected rivers and parks are critical for their survival.

Behavior:
Social, living in groups called pods of 10–30 individuals. Mostly nocturnal grazers, spending days in water to regulate body temperature. Males are territorial, marking boundaries in water.

Diet and Feeding Habits:
Herbivorous, feeding on grasses at night. They consume 35–50 kg of vegetation daily, contributing to nutrient cycling in rivers and floodplains.

Reproduction System:
Breeding occurs year-round. Females give birth to 1 calf after 8 months, usually in water. Calves can swim immediately and are protected by mothers and pod members.

Life Cycle and Longevity:
Hippos live 40–50 years. Sexual maturity occurs at 5–7 years. Social bonds help protect calves and maintain territory hierarchies.

Predators and Threats:
Humans are the main threat. Crocodiles and lions rarely prey on adults. Habitat destruction and hunting for meat and ivory teeth affect populations.

Ecological Role / Usefulness:
Hippos maintain aquatic ecosystems by controlling vegetation, creating channels in rivers, and providing habitats for fish and birds. Their dung fertilizes aquatic ecosystems.

Cultural and Historical Significance:
Hippos feature in Ethiopian folklore and are symbols of strength and water power. They attract tourism and educational interest.

Conservation Status:
Vulnerable. Protected areas and waterway conservation are crucial.

Interesting Facts:
Hippos can hold their breath up to 5 minutes underwater. Despite their size, they can run surprisingly fast on land, up to 30 km/h.
